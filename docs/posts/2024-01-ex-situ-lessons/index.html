What does it take to establish the world’s first ex-situ population of a critically endangered heron?

Conservation Science
Conservation Breeding
Bhutan
In April 2021 we collected two pre-fledged White-bellied Heron chicks from a wild nest in Bhutan. Here’s what we learned from building the world’s only captive population of this species.
Author

Indra P. Acharja

Published

January 15, 2024

In April 2021, my colleagues and I stood in a forest beside the Mangdechhu river in Bhutan and made a decision that had never been made before for this species: we were going to collect two chicks from a wild nest and transfer them to a newly constructed Conservation Center.

It was not a decision taken lightly. Removing individuals from a wild population that numbers fewer than 60 is a serious intervention. But the IUCN Species Survival Commission had assessed the situation and concluded that the risk of inaction — losing the entire wild population before we could establish any safety net — was greater than the risk of the collection itself. We had a facility. We had a plan. We had the government’s support. We went ahead.

Why ex-situ conservation?

The White-bellied Heron faces a trajectory that conservation biologists recognize as dangerous: a small, fragmented population with declining reproductive success, restricted to a narrow geographic range, facing multiple simultaneous threats. The wild population alone is not a secure future for the species.

An ex-situ population serves several purposes. First, it is an insurance policy: if something catastrophic happens to the wild population — a disease outbreak, a bad drought, a sudden collapse in prey — there is a gene pool that can anchor a recovery program. Second, a captive population allows researchers to study aspects of biology that are nearly impossible to examine in the wild: breeding behaviour, dietary requirements, disease susceptibility, growth rates. Third, birds raised in captivity, if managed well, can eventually become founders for reintroduction programs into restored or newly secured wild habitats.

What we found out

The first years of the Conservation Center were as much a learning experience as a success story. White-bellied Herons had never been kept in captivity before. There were no established husbandry protocols, no nutritional guidelines, no guidance from other institutions because no other institution had attempted this.

We worked closely with veterinarians and with zoo professionals who had experience with related heron species. We experimented with diet — the birds are specialist fish-eaters in the wild, and finding a captive diet that met their nutritional needs while being practically manageable was a significant challenge. We designed enclosures that balanced the birds’ need for space and environmental complexity with the need for health monitoring.

The birds survived. They grew. By 2022, the first pair had begun showing breeding behaviour in captivity — a milestone nobody had achieved before with this species.

What comes next

The Conservation Center is now a functioning facility, but it is still young. The immediate priorities are: expanding the captive population carefully; developing robust health monitoring protocols; and beginning the long process of developing reintroduction science — identifying which wild sites might be suitable for future release, and what habitat restoration would need to happen first.

It will be a long process. Ex-situ conservation programs for birds typically operate over decades before reintroduction becomes feasible. But the alternative — watching the last wild population dwindle without any backup — was not an option we were prepared to accept.

The two chicks we collected in 2021 are now adults. They are alive. That, for now, is enough.
