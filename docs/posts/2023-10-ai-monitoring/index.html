Using AI to find the world’s rarest heron in thousands of camera trap images

Technology
AI
Monitoring
Camera traps deployed along remote Himalayan rivers generate thousands of images. Finding a handful of White-bellied Heron detections in that data used to take weeks. AI-based detection is changing that.
Author

Indra P. Acharja

Published

October 5, 2023

A single camera trap deployed along a Himalayan riverbank for one month will generate somewhere between 500 and 5,000 images, depending on how much wildlife passes by and how sensitive the trigger is set. Most of those images will be of nothing useful: wind-blown vegetation, passing cattle, the occasional goat. A small fraction will show the birds and mammals you deployed the camera for.

Finding those images — and within them, identifying the White-bellied Heron — used to be entirely manual work. A researcher would sit at a laptop and click through image after image, tagging and categorising. For a survey covering multiple sites across two river basins, this could mean weeks of processing time before any analysis could begin.

AI-based image classification is changing this, and we have been working to apply it to White-bellied Heron monitoring as part of my doctoral research at Texas State University.

The challenge with rare species

Training a machine learning model to recognise a bird requires images — many images — of that bird in a variety of poses, lighting conditions, and backgrounds. For common species, this is not a problem: there are hundreds of thousands of labelled bird photographs available in public datasets.

For the White-bellied Heron, there are perhaps a few hundred good photographs in existence globally. This is not a lot of training data. The species is large, distinctive, and unmistakable to a human observer, but training a reliable classifier on sparse data requires careful methodological choices: transfer learning from models trained on related species, data augmentation to artificially expand the training set, and careful validation to ensure the model generalises to new field conditions.

What we have built so far

Working with colleagues at Texas State, we have developed a pipeline that takes raw camera trap images, runs them through a classifier to flag images likely to contain large waterbirds, and then presents those flagged images to a human reviewer for confirmation. The system doesn’t replace human judgment — it assists it.

In practice, this means that instead of reviewing 4,000 images manually, a researcher reviews perhaps 200 flagged images. The risk of false negatives (missing a real heron detection) is managed by keeping the classifier’s threshold conservative — it is better to flag 200 images than to miss a genuine sighting.

Early tests in the Punatsangchhu suggest the pipeline reduces processing time by around 80%, with detection rates comparable to careful manual review.

The bigger picture

The value of this work extends beyond any single survey. One of the persistent challenges for White-bellied Heron conservation is the cost and logistical difficulty of monitoring a wide-ranging species across remote terrain in multiple countries. Bhutan, India, and Myanmar all have territory within the heron’s range; coordinating consistent survey effort across all of them is difficult.

Scalable, low-cost monitoring technology — camera traps, acoustic sensors, satellite imagery — could make it possible for range-country partners to contribute systematically to population monitoring without the need for large, expensive field teams. AI-assisted processing makes the data from those tools actually manageable.

We are still in early stages. But the potential to build a network of low-cost sensors across the White-bellied Heron’s range, feeding into a shared database that tracks population trends across countries, is real. That kind of data would transform our ability to assess the species’ status and respond to threats as they emerge.
